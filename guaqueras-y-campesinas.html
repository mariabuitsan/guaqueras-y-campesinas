Acerca del proyecto

The Patricia Emerald is praised at the Museum of Natural History as one of the most astonishing uncut emeralds known worldwide. The Crown of the Andes at the Met features 400 untreated colonial emeralds as a testament to human craftsmanship and, one could also add, a testament to the coloniality paradoxes. The precious stones encrusted on the artifacts, and plenty of others found in museums or jewelry stores worldwide, have been extracted from Western Boyacá, Colombia. The ones I went to visit vaguely mentioned the people from this territory. The glory of the artifacts and the gems –or the glory ascribed to them– has consistently concealed the glory of its people, those who excavate minerals inside fiery tunnels deep within the mountains. It has also obscured the complex material realities, with all their poignant unequal details and consequences, often created and reproduced in extractivist economies.Our reverence for these items hides away the pain, but also the joy that intersects the humans, the places, the labors, the affects, and the other objects and epistemologies flourishing in this region.
The Guaqueras and Campesinas digital collection, thus, aspires to present with creativity and, through an experimental digital humanities methodology, this other angle of the story. It also intends to pay homage to unnoticed rich entanglements weaved through and by the women born or currently residing in these municipalities.
Much of the scholarly research on western Boyacá has focused on disputes over emerald mines, land ownership, and the violence caused and made profitable due to its trade. These approaches highlight masculine figures and masculinized narratives. Although all of these studies are critical and continue to be essential for historical and national memory purposes, especially after Colombia’s Peace Agreement (2016) and the establishment of the Truth Commission, there are two main reasons why the Guaqueras and Campesinas collection is valuable and adds to this immense effort of narrating the multiple truths about our conflict in a different vein. First, the initial scholarly work privileged an economic and sociopolitical analysis, centering on History's “big” themes, where women were often put to the side or appeared in the shadows.
Second, non-institutional approaches to national history and collective memory allow us to focus on micro details and “non-factual” narratives, bringing to light many valuable ideas and affective registers.

Guaqueras y Campesinas del occidente de Boyacá

Colección digital sobre las historias y memorias de las mujeres de los municipios del occidente de Boyacá, Colombia.

Digital collection documnting the stories and memories of women from Western Boyacá, Colombia

Claudia Vargas, Martha Ramos, Rubiela Ruiz, Silvia Bernal, Yolima Cruz

Esta colección digital es un trabajo en proceso, en el cual queremos documentar la vida de las mujeres que viven, nuetren y protegen los territorios del Occidente de Boyacá. No pretende ser un colección sistemática ni exahustiva. Es un espacio para conocer un poco la vida diaria de las mujeres que en los municipios esmeraldíferos de Colombia se enfrentan a grandes desafíos con una sensibilidad única. Les invitamos a explorar fotos, entrevistas y objetos que hemos venido documentando con la intención de generar registros digitales para indagar en el pasado y reflexionar sobre el presente y el futuro.

Guaqueras

Guaqueras es un sustantivo femenino. En el contexto de Boyacá, identifica a las mineras artesanales y de pequeña escala. Según la Real Academia Española, el sustantivo se deriva del término quechua waca. Una ‘waca’ se refiere a los tesoros u objetos sagrados y a los lugares físicos donde se colocaban estos valiosos artefactos. A menudo, las wacas se encontraban en sitios ceremoniales o tumbas indígenas, exaltando su significado cultural y espiritual. La ‘waca’ es a la vez un objeto y un lugar.
Guaqueras is a feminine noun in Spanish. In Boyacá’s context, it identifies artisan and low-scale diggers and miners. According to the Royal Spanish Academy, the noun is derived from the Quechua term waca. A ‘waca’ refers to treasures or sacred objects and the physical locations where these valuable items were placed. Often, wacas were placed in ceremonial sites or indigenous tombs, exalting their cultural and spiritual significance. The ‘waca’ is simultaneously an object and a place.

Campesinas

Campesinas, también un sustantivo femenino, agrupa mujeres campesinas, productoras o agricultoras. A lo largo de la historia en Colombia se aprecia un proceso de simbiosis —a veces de colaboración, otras de confrontación— entre estos dos grupos, cuya identidad política y cultural ha estado determinada en gran medida por su trabajo con y para la tierra.
Campesinas, also a feminine noun, means women peasants or farmers. Colombia's history reveals a long process of symbiosis—at times collaborative, at others confrontational—between these two groups, whose political and cultural identities are largely shaped by their labor.

Occidente de Boyacá

Western Boyacá is composed of 15 municipalities, and the emerald-producing zone “is divided into 11 municipalities” (Leiteritz & Riaño, 2020, p.289). The material included now in the digital collection is only from women living in one municipality, San Pablo de Borbur. According to Colombia’s 2018 National Census the majority of the households in the region has no access to internet. For example, in San Pablo de Borbur, 96.6% of the surveyed homes reported no internet access (DANE, 2018).
